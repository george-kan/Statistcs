Why do we need an estimator?

In the previous post we discussed statistical models and the idea that a statistical model is a pair \({\left(S, \{ P_\theta \} _{\theta \in \Theta }\right)}\). We also discussed the fact that if our model is well specified then the true distribution (the one that governs reality) will be included in our selected family of distributions \(P_{\theta \in \Theta}\) for certain \(\theta\). But we did not discuss at all how we can estimate this \(\theta\). This is were estimators come into play.

Suppose we have a true distribution with the true parameter \(\color{blue}\theta\) and we can obtain sample data from that distribution. Our goal is to be able to estimate quantities of the true distribution such as \(\color{blue}\theta\), its mean or its median by using the sample data. In particular, what we need is a function that takes as input the sample data from the true distribution and outputs a “guess” or an estimate for the quantity of the true distribution we are interested in.

As an example, suppose the true distribution is a Poisson with lambda = 2 (lambda is unknown to us) and we can obtain sample data as seen in the picture below.
What would be a good guess for lambda given the sample data?

To answer this question we have to think a bit about our distribution. If \(X\) follows a Poisson distribution with parameter lambda, then we know that the mean of \(X\) equals lambda (\(E[X] = \lambda\)). That gives a hint as to what would be a good estimator for lambda, the sample mean. And looking at the chart above, we see that as the sample size goes bigger, the sample mean tends goes to 2 which is indeed the lambda of the true distribution.

But are we sure that it is a good estimator? What if it was by chance? Can we quantify how good an estimator is?
